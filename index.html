About

I'm a UX researcher and PhD candidate in Human-Computer Interaction at the University of Cape Town, specializing in community-based participatory design in African contexts. My work bridges academic research rigor with practical product design, focusing on healthcare, education, and AI ethics in under-resourced environments.

With 5+ years of field research experience across South African communities, I bring deep qualitative expertise combined with user-centered design methods. My research has been published in peer-reviewed journals including PLoS ONE and ACM proceedings, and I've led projects ranging from mobile health apps to AI literacy initiatives.

I'm passionate about designing technology that respects community knowledge, builds on existing social structures, and addresses real needs rather than assumed problems. My approach combines Ubuntu philosophy with participatory design methods to create ethical, sustainable solutions.

When I'm not conducting research, I experiment with film photography, garden, and read campus novels. I also maintain a book blog where I write about literature and ideas.

TB Screening App for Healthcare Workers

Context: User-centered redesign of mobile tuberculosis screening tool for Cape Town clinics

Role: Lead UX Researcher | Duration: 6 months | Methods: Contextual inquiry (n=10), usability testing, iterative prototyping (3 iterations)

The Challenge

Healthcare workers in Cape Town townships needed to assess tuberculin skin tests for TB screening, but the existing app had a complex interface, required capturing 9 images, and had never been tested with actual users. Workers operated in under-resourced clinics with less than 5 minutes per patient.

Key Insights

  • Time scarcity dominated all other concerns — any feature requiring more than 2-3 minutes wouldn't be adopted
  • Visual instructions vastly outperformed text — replacing lengthy text with pictorial guidance and video improved comprehension dramatically
  • Context-specific concerns emerged — CHWs raised safety concerns (device theft), data privacy questions, and language preferences

Solution & Impact

Through 3 design iterations, I: (1) Replaced text with pictorial instructions and video with audio, (2) Improved color contrast and added scrolling indicators, (3) Reduced from 9 to 5 critical images with real-time guidance overlays.

Usability Score: <3.0 on PSSUQ (1-7 scale)

Validation: Published in PLoS ONE

What I learned: The value of proxy testing (using students before busy end-users), and that context-specific factors (safety, time, language) often matter more than traditional usability metrics.

Maternal Health Information Needs Study

Context: Community-based participatory research exploring health information seeking in rural South Africa

Role: Lead Researcher | Duration: 18 months | Methods: In-depth interviews (n=25), co-design workshops (n=3), participatory observation

The Challenge

Digital health solutions for maternal care often fail in rural African contexts because they're designed based on assumptions about individual information needs and smartphone access. I needed to understand how health information actually circulated in communities to inform better technology design.

Key Insights

  • Information needs are communal, not individual — most mothers (18 of 25) sought information on behalf of absent family members
  • Trust in health information comes from community validation — not app credentials or medical authority
  • Digital literacy and infrastructure constraints shape access patterns — offline-first and feature phone compatibility are non-negotiable

Impact

Framework for community-centered maternal health technology design. Ongoing research partnership with community. Findings published in ACM CSCW and informed CoMaCH Network recommendations.

What I learned: Building trust takes time but yields richer, more honest data. Consent must be renegotiated continuously as relationships deepen, not just collected once at the start.

AI Literacy Workshops

Context: Speculative design workshops exploring AI impacts with under-resourced communities in Cape Town

Role: Lead Facilitator & Researcher | Duration: 6 months | Methods: Speculative design workshops, story circles, thematic analysis

The Challenge

AI technologies are being deployed in South African contexts without community input or awareness. I designed workshops to help community members understand AI, deliberate on its impacts, and collectively imagine alternative futures.

Key Insights

  • AI concerns centered on job displacement, not privacy — different from typical Western framing of AI risks
  • Community members spoke for absent others — design must account for collective rather than individual decision-making
  • Trust in AI tied to local control and transparency — not technical sophistication or performance metrics

Impact

Workshop framework for AI literacy in under-resourced contexts. Community-generated speculative scenarios informing ethical AI design. Insights contributing to Ubuntu-informed AI ethics framework (PhD work in progress).

What I learned: Speculative design methods help communities engage with abstract technical concepts. Facilitating difficult conversations about technology futures requires holding space for uncertainty and collective reasoning.
